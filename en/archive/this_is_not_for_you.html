Two musical projects caught my attention in the past monts. They are not directly related to each other, but in their essence they have something very much in common. I am referring to the recent activities of the Más Tiempo label, which is run by Skepta & Jammer (in the following lines I will focus on my view in the context of Skepta, because of the mentioned pair he is closer to me and I can better describe my thoughts on his example) and the album New Blue Sun by Andre3000.

 

We can shamelessely call both performers rap legends. There are a lot of articles on this topic on the internet so just in a nutshell: Skepta is a long-time pioneer of Grime music and has a lot to do with the current shape of UK rap. Andre3000, whom most people will know primarily as a member of Outkast, is an OG for more than one generation.

 

In addition to the mythical status of both artists, the mentioned projects have something else in common – paradoxically, both of them push their status aside and instead focus on their feelings. As a result they both created something unexpected, but also very authentic.

Skepta's label Más Tiempo releases music, but also hosts parties or DJ sets –and all this, surprisingly, in the genre of house music. Despite the fact that Skepta has a solid foundation in DJing and music production, the average fan would not expect a similar sound from him. As Skepta himself mentioned in the interview, he essentialy leaves the microphone aside in these cases.

 

The first swallows flew by earlier, but it was the single Can't Play Myself (A Tribute to Amy) that caught my attention. As a DJ, I was delighted such a great club track came out of Skepta's production – and a whole compilation after that. But that wasn't the only thing that caught my attention. House rhythms suddenly bring a completely new atmosphere, where the roughness of grime music is replaced by melodies and the faces of the participants are all smiles.

 

In the midst of preparing a new rap album, Skepta has found time to make a detour that may not appeal to all existing fans. However, he shows his skills in a new light and momentarily shifts his attention, and the attention of those who are willing to, completely elsewhere & I'm here for it.

 

The mindset around this project is perfectly captured in the said interview for Capital Dance. Anyway, it's hard to get a complete picture without listening to the music. I recommend playing at least the compilation Más Tiempo vol. 1 and the b2b DJ set for Mixmag.

Andre3000's new album was highly anticipated as it was supposed to be his solo debut even after his long rap career. The fact that the album was released, but it has nothing to do with rap, is all the more important. Instead, it's an instrumental album with wind instruments leading the way. Excuse me?

 

There were some hints here, but thanks to a few feats in recent years, it wasn't so clear. In an interview Andre says “What should I rap about?” and it makes sense. He says he's the happiest when he's creating. But rap isn't an authentic form of creation for him right now. When I hear him playing the flute and talking about the new album, I feel a lot of sincerity and passion in it. Given his position, I can imagine it needs quite a lot of courage to make this decision. Thanks to it,  though, he can escape from the anxious captivity of fame, to be outside, meet people and create.

 

To the disappointment of some fans, another "Hey Ya" is not happening. However, I am happy that someone can elegantly reflect their life development in their work and move where the wind blows them. Respect.

 

I definitely recommend listening to the whole album as well as the recording of the performance from The Late Show. The inspiration and source for a deeper understanding of the album was this interview, in which Andre also talks about his career and reveals some personal things.

Both projects receive a lot of praise, but also criticism – perhaps because neither artist compares to the “greats” in those respective genres, or simply because these projects do not reach the same “quality” as the rap of both artists. If their critics (often fans) had a little more empathy, they might realize that none of the performers is doing it with the goal of being (again) the best. From the interviews and videos for both projects it is clear that they a) don't have to and b) do it for fun, because they want to – in short, they do it for themselves.


I would like to see more artists with similar projects in the future, in which they are not afraid to leave the constraints of their usual work, even if it means a total departure from what is expected of them.
